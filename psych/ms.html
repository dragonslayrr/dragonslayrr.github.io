Multiple Sclerosis
Wernickes Aphasia
Description:
Multiple Sclerosis also known as MS is considered an autoimmune disease. Multiple Sclerosis results in the body’s immune system attacking the brain, optic nerve, and spinal cords nerve cell’s myelin sheath.
Causes:
There is no known cause of Multiple Sclerosis. While the cause is unknown there are many risk factors that can affect your chance of getting multiple sclerosis. These risk factors include:
  • Age
  • Sex
  • Family history
  • Certain infections
  • Race
  • Climate
  • Vitamin D.
  • Certain autoimmune diseases
  • Smoking
More details about how these factors can affect your risk of contracting multiple sclerosis at Mayo Clinic.
Diagnosis:
There is no specific way to diagnose Multiple Sclerosis so doctors have to rule out other conditions that can produce similar signs and symptoms. This process is known as a differential diagnosis. Some diagnoses that may be used to identify MS are blood tests, spinal taps, MRI’s, and evoked potential tests.
Symptoms:
Multiple Sclerosis has many symptoms, some more common than others. Some of these symptoms include:
  • double vision
  • fatigue
  • numbness and tingling
  • muscle spasms
  • stiffness
  • weakness
  • mobility issues
  • generalized pain
  • bladder and bowel problems
  • difficulty swallowing and speaking
  • depression and anxiety
  • etc.
Who is affected:
Multiple sclerosis can affect anyone but there are some people who are more likely to get diagnosed with MS. The main two general factors for contracting MS are age and gender. The age factor is that people between the ages of 20 and 50 are more likely to be diagnosed with MS. The gender factor is that women are 3 times as likely to get MS as men are.
What is the outlook:
Multiple Sclerosis is rarely fatal but you can get serious complications from a severe case of MS. The average life expectancy for people with MS is around 5 to 10 years lower than average but this gap appears to keep getting smaller.
Treatment:
There is no cure to multiple sclerosis so the treatment focuses on allowing the patient to quickly recover from attacks, slowing the disease's progression, and managing the symptoms. If the symptoms are mild, treatment may not be necessary. Relapsing Multiple Sclerosis (RMS) can be treated with prescription drugs like KESIMPTA.
