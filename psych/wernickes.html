Wernickes Aphasia
Description
The main characteristic of Wernicke's aphasia is impaired language comprehension. Despite having impaired comprehension someone with Wernicke's aphasia may still have normal rate, rythem, and grammar.
Causes:
Wernicke’s aphasia is caused by brain damage usually on the left side. This is often caused by a loss of blood flow due to a stroke. The most common way to get Wernicke’s aphasia is from an ischemic stroke which affects the posterior temporal lobe of the dominant hemisphere.
Diagnosis:
Wernicke’s aphasia is typically diagnosed through brain imaging tests like MRI or CT scan, this helps the doctor determine if other parts of the brain were also damaged.
Symptoms:
Typical symptoms of Wernicke’s aphasia include stringing words together to form nonsense sentences, making up words, me unaware of mistakes in their speech, having difficulty repeating phrases, adding words when trying to repeat someone, and interrupting others and speaking rapidly. Victims of Wernicke’s aphasia may also have severely impared reading and writing ability, understanding visual material better than written or spoken words, and they may maintain cognitive abilities other than those associated with language.
Who is affected:
Wernicke’s aphasia commonly affects those who have frequent strokes.
What is the outlook:
Someone who is suffering from wernicke’s aphasia can recover with the proper treatment and speech therapy to regain what they lost.
Treatment:
While Wernicke’s aphasia has no standard treatment it can be cured or partly cured through speech therapy.
